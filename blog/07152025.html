Why MLOps is a necessary skill for all Data Scientists

Date: July 15, 2025 | Author: Venkat Vivek

The journey of a data professional is an exciting one, but it's also a path of constant evolution. We start with data cleaning and exploratory analysis, move on to building models, and then, often, we hit a wall. We have a fantastic model that performs beautifully in our local environment, but how do we get it into the hands of users? This is where the world of MLOps comes into play, and why it's a necessary skill for every data scientist.

The Trajectory from Data Science to MLOps

A traditional data scientist focuses on the experimental phase of a machine learning project. This includes identifying the problem, collecting and cleaning data, feature engineering, and model training and evaluation. For a long time, this was the end of the line for many. The model was built, and then it would be handed off, often with a prayer, to a different team to figure out deployment.

However, as businesses have become more reliant on machine learning for critical decisions, this hand-off approach has proven to be inefficient and prone to failure. The trajectory of a modern data professional now extends beyond the local notebook. It leads to the production phase, which involves tasks like containerization, model deployment, and monitoring. This shift is not about leaving data science behind, but about embracing the full lifecycle of a machine learning model.

What is an MLOps Engineer?

An MLOps engineer is the person who bridges the gap between the data science team and the operations team. They take a data scientist's model and make it accessible to the software that uses it in a real-world application. They build, manage, and optimize the infrastructure and pipelines that automate the entire machine learning lifecycle, from training to deployment and continuous monitoring. This role is a hybrid of machine learning, software engineering, and DevOps.

Their key responsibilities include:

  • Model Deployment: Packaging models and deploying them to production environments.
  • Automation: Building automated pipelines for model training and deployment.
  • Monitoring: Tracking model performance, data drift, and system health in production.
  • Containerization: Using technologies like Docker to ensure models run consistently across different environments.
  • Collaboration: Acting as a liaison between data scientists and software engineers.

The Value of MLOps in the Job Market

The job market is trending toward a demand for professionals who can not only build models but also ensure they are reliable, scalable, and maintainable in a production environment. The global MLOps market is experiencing explosive growth, projected to be worth billions in the coming years. This is creating a high demand for skilled MLOps engineers, and the salary for these roles reflects their value. A professional with a solid understanding of both data science and MLOps principles has a significant competitive advantage.

Learning MLOps: A Data Scientist's Perspective

For a data scientist, transitioning into MLOps is a natural progression. You already have the foundational knowledge of machine learning. The next step is to expand your skill set to include the operational aspects.

Here's how a data scientist can start learning MLOps:

  1. Understand DevOps Principles: Familiarize yourself with concepts like version control (Git), continuous integration (CI), and continuous deployment (CD).
  2. Learn Containerization: Get hands-on with Docker. Learn how to containerize a simple Python application or a machine learning model.
  3. Explore Cloud Platforms: Become proficient with one of the major cloud providers (AWS, Google Cloud, or Azure). Most MLOps work happens in the cloud.
  4. Build an End-to-End Project: The best way to learn is by doing. Take a personal project and build a complete pipeline, from data ingestion to model deployment and monitoring.

By building on your existing data science knowledge and adding these new skills, you can become a more well-rounded and valuable professional, ready to take on the next big challenge in the world of machine learning.
